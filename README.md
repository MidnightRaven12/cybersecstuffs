## Philosophy

We mainly believe in the Hacker Ethic in its original form as denoted by Stephen Levy in the book: *Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution*. 

It is as follows:

1. Access to computers—and anything which might teach you something about the way the world works—should be unlimited and total.

The main idea behind this is to give access to the mechanisms behind making something, whether it is tearing apart some piece of software to learn how it works, or tearing apart an alarm clock to see how it is engineered. It can be thought of "Access aids the expansion of technology."

2. All information should be free.

This is (1), except with ideas rather than physical things.

3.  Mistrust authority - Promote Decentralization

We believe that bureaucracies in any form are flawed systems, and that they will slow the expansion of knowledge. The best way to promote the free exchange of information is to present no boundaries between a hacker and a piece of information.

4. Hackers should be judged by their hacking, not bogus criteria such as degrees, age, race, sex, or position.


Hacker skill, curiosity, and passion are the three sole factors in determining whether you are accepted in the community. Anything else (including certifications, and other qualifications) are deemed ireelevant.

5. You can create art and beauty on a computer.

Code has a natural beauty. For example: many coding languages (such as [BASH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bash_(Unix_shell))) has a way of writing a one line [reverse shell](https://www.imperva.com/learn/application-security/reverse-shell/).  Code also gives you the power to make many different applications, such as the games that you play.

6. Computers can change your life for the better. 

We believe that computers are a revolutionary invention, allowing us to communicate from across the world, and make scientific progress on a scale never seen before. While people are scared of things like AI taking over the world, it is only us that can program and stop these systems from taking over. 

Unfortunately, there are a few rules that we have to implement.

## Few Basic Rules

1. **DO NOT USE ANY CODE THAT HASN'T BEEN VERIFIED TO BE SAFE.**  There have been several instances where people copy malicious code from websites on the internet, only to find their own computer taken over. (See [Script Kid](https://www.youtube.com/c/ScriptKid) for the effects as to what happens when you download game cheats.) This doesn't prevent you from copying and pasting code and seeing what it does. However, you have to do it with caution.
2. **Be Reasonable.** There are actual viruses and ransomware attacks on this written by some exceptional students. Don't go and use them against other people without their permission, and don't go crazy. Hacking is a [**felony charge**](https://www.findlaw.com/criminal/criminal-charges/hacking-laws-and-punishments.html) under the CFAA which carries up to 10 years in your first conviction and 20 years in your second. Back in the early days of the internet, hacking was relatively easy, and there was almost nothing to stop you. Nowadays, almost any company that you would find would have a tight cybersecurity program ready to stop any threats, and it takes a lot more knowledge to hack into a company undetected than a few decades ago. Put simply

> There's two parts to hacking. One is the actual breaching of security. The second is hiding your breach. Movies only show part one through visuals, and never really show part 2. This is especially crucial when you want to maintain access. If you're going to hack, you better learn how to do it properly. 

A few weeks of looking at code won't suffice. It takes years in order to hack your first company, and then it'll become easier from here on out. 

3. Extending on to this: make sure that you don't put any obscene content on here, and make sure that all conversations are productive. Everyone can see what you post here, and if you have an issue with a specific person using it: just go and talk to that person directly.   
4. As for developers: make sure to guard against this by making sure that the person who is going to be using your script has the knowledge of the programming language that you are going to be using.
5. Hacking is allowed. However: what you may not do is to gain the personal information, or compromise any systems belonging to other students or the school unless you gain written permission. Again: just ask. 
6. Give credit where credit is due. Be reasonable about it. A good rule of thumb is to give credit to the main author if two or three non-standard functions in code are used. 
7. All major merge requests go to Mr. Ballard (or whoever is the teacher at the time). Basically, you just fork the repository (make a local copy) and make changes to it, and when you're done, just send it back.
8. Do not upload answers to specific assignments on here. You may give hints, but never actual answers.
